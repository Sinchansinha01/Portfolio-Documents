Project 03 · Public Sector Analytics

Government Infrastructure Analytics Dashboard

Period: January – April 2025 Domain: Public Sector · Self-Service BI Tools: SQL · Power BI · Power Query · DAX
Section 01

Business Problem

Government infrastructure spending is large, complex, and highly scrutinized. Taxpayers, elected officials, and oversight bodies all expect accountability — not just that funds were spent, but that they were spent effectively, on time, and in alignment with stated priorities.

The challenge this project addressed is one that affects public sector organizations broadly: data exists, but it is fragmented. Funding allocation records sit in one system, project timelines in another, and performance metrics in spreadsheets managed by individual teams. When leadership needs to understand how investments are tracking — or when stakeholders request a status update — someone has to spend days manually pulling, cleaning, and assembling information from multiple sources.

"The absence of a reliable, centralized reporting solution created operational drag, slowed decision-making, and made it difficult to demonstrate accountability to external stakeholders managing large capital programs."

The core business questions this project set out to answer:

  • How are approved infrastructure funds being allocated across project categories and regions?
  • Which projects are on schedule, which are delayed, and what is the magnitude of those delays?
  • Are performance metrics aligning with commitments made to stakeholders and oversight bodies?
  • Can leadership access this information without requesting a custom report every time?
Section 02

Solution Approach

The project began with data consolidation. Infrastructure data covering funding allocations, project milestones, timeline tracking, and performance outcomes was brought together and cleaned using SQL. Government datasets carry particular data quality challenges — inconsistent naming conventions, incomplete milestone records, funding categories that don't map cleanly across fiscal years. SQL was used to standardize these inconsistencies, creating a reliable base dataset before any visualization work began.

A key design principle throughout was scalability. Rather than building a dashboard that served one team's current needs and required manual updates as data changed, the solution was architected as a self-service model — meaning as new project data is added to the underlying sources, the dashboard updates automatically. For government operations where project portfolios span years, this transforms the dashboard from a static snapshot into a living operational tool.

The Power BI dashboard was structured around three analytical layers. First: a funding allocation view showing how approved budgets were distributed across project types, regions, and program categories. Second: a project timeline tracker visualizing scheduled versus actual delivery dates — making delays visible at a glance and flagging which projects required escalation. Third: a performance metrics layer tracking KPIs that reflected whether projects were delivering on their stated outcomes, not just financial commitments.

Stakeholder transparency was a primary design goal. The visual language — color coding for on-track versus delayed projects, summary indicators at portfolio level, drill-down capability for individual project details — was designed to make information accessible to non-technical reviewers including elected officials, department heads, and external auditors.

Results & Business Impact

Stakeholder accountability and transparency

The self-service architecture was the most operationally significant outcome. Reporting that previously required days of data preparation could now be accessed in minutes. For a government team managing multiple concurrent projects with ongoing stakeholder obligations, this represented a substantial reduction in administrative burden — freeing analyst capacity for higher-value work rather than data assembly.

Timeline visibility created a new accountability mechanism. When delays became visible in the dashboard — rather than surfacing only when someone escalated — leadership could intervene earlier. Early intervention on infrastructure projects typically reduces the cost and complexity of recovery.

The transparency framework strengthened the team's ability to communicate with external stakeholders. Performance reviews, funding justifications, and oversight reporting became more evidence-based — grounded in consistent, verifiable data rather than manually compiled status updates.
